\section{Problem 1.}

A trapezoid has consecutive sides of length $8$, $10$, and $12$ in that order. 
Find the largest possible area of such a trapezoid rounded down to the nearest integer.

\begin{definition}
    \textbf{AREA OF A TRAPEZOID}
    \begin{equation}
        A = \frac{a+b}{2}h
    \end{equation}
\end{definition}

\begin{solution}
When approaching an optimization problem, we are going to create an 
area function and evaluate the vertices. In this geometry problem, it 
important to realize that there are two possible variations on how we 
can create a trapezoid.

We can either create a Slant-Base-Slant or a Base-Slant-Base trapezoid. Our
first problem is to decide whether each case gives out a larger area. Let us 
first start with the Base-Slant-Base case. Since we have fixed bases, we can simply
use our definition for area of a trapezoid. To maximize the area also implies 
maxing out the height if and only if the bases are fixed. More formally, we can
describe it with mathematical terms.

For a fixed $a, b \in \mathbb{R}^+$, it implies the following:
\begin{align*}
     A(h) &= \frac{a+b}{2}h \\
  \Longrightarrow   \argmax_{h} A &= \argmax_{h} h
\end{align*}

From this, we can logically find the maximum area for our Base-Slant-Base trapezoid
by making it a right trapezoid.
\begin{align*}
    a &= 8, \ b = 12, \ h = 10 \\
    A &= \frac{a+b}{2}h \\
    A_{bsb \ maxed}  &= \frac{8+12}{2}10 \\
\Longrightarrow    A_{bsb \ maxed} &= 100 \ \text{units}^{2}
\end{align*}

Our next case is the Slant-Base-Slant trapezoid. Something to note is that if we were 
to maximize height, we can show that the area of a Slant-Base-Slant trapezoid is larger than the 
area of a Base-Slant-Base trapezoid. This means that our answer is actually optimizing the 
Slant-Base-Slant triangle.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.4\linewidth]{maxheightsbs.jpg}
\end{figure}
\begin{align*}
    s_{1} &= 8, \ a = 10, s_{2} = 12 \\
    A_{sbs} &= 8 \cdot 10 + \frac{8 \cdot (\sqrt{12^{2} - 8^{2}})}{2} \\
      &= 80 + 4 \sqrt{16 \cdot 5} \\
      &= 80 + 16 \sqrt{5} \\
    A_{sbs}  &\approx 115.777 \ \text{units}^{2} \\
    \Longrightarrow & \boxed{A_{sbs} > A_{bsb \ maxed}}
\end{align*}

\subsection{Optimizing Slant-Base-Slant Trapezoid}

Just as a sanity check, it is imperative that we define the bounding range of 
the opposite base. If we were to collapse the two slants into a line, we would approach a 
max base would be $30$. With the opposing argument, if we were to connect the two slants 
into a point, our minimum base would be $0$. Using Heron's Formula, we have a "minimum" area of
$A = \sqrt{s(s-a)(s-b)(s-c)} = \sqrt{15(15-8)(15-10)(15-12)} = 15\sqrt{7} \approx 39.7 \ \text{units}^{2}$.
All of this information should give us a base to work with.

In hindsight, I'd like to work with area function that is dependent on height. We know off the bat
that it is restricted from $0 < x \leq 8$ from the triangle approach and from maximizing one of the 
slant leg height. We can solve for the other base in terms of the height using the Pythagorean Theorem 
and some substitutions. After which, we can create our area function and maximize it.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \begin{minipage}[h]{0.48\textwidth}
        \vspace{0pt}
        \includegraphics[width=1\linewidth]{heightdrawing.jpg}
        \caption{Slant-Base-Slant Trapezoid}
    \end{minipage}
    \begin{minipage}[h]{0.48\textwidth}
        \vspace{0pt}
        \begin{align*}
            & A = \frac{a+b}{2}h \\
            & A = \frac{10 + b}{2}h, \quad b = \sqrt{64-h^{2}}+10+\sqrt{144-h^{2}} \\
            & A(h) = \frac{10+\left(\sqrt{64-h^{2}}+10+\sqrt{144-h^{2}}\right)}{2}h, \quad 0 < h \leq 8 \\
            & A'(h) = 10+\frac{\sqrt{64-h^{2}}}{2}-\frac{h^{2}}{2\sqrt{64-h^{2}}} \\
            & \hspace{1.5cm} +\frac{\sqrt{144-h^{2}}}{2}-\frac{h^{2}}{2\sqrt{144-h^{2}}} \\
            & A'(h) = 0 \Longrightarrow h_{max \ area} \approx 7.6552 \\
            & A(h_{max \ area}) \approx 120.816 \\
            & \Longrightarrow \boxed{\lfloor A(h_{max \ area}) \rfloor = 120}
        \end{align*}
    \end{minipage}
\end{figure}



We are kind of forced to use numerical methods as solving for when deriviative
is equal to 0 would be quite the algebraic hassle. Another way would be to 
floor the entire $A(h)$ function and simply graph values.

the largest possible area rounded down to the nearest integer of \boxed{$120 $\text{units}^{2}}. 

\end{solution}
